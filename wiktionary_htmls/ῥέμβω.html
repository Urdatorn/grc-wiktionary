Ancient Greek

[edit]

Etymology

[edit]

From Proto-Indo-European *wremb-, from *werb- (“to turn, twist, bend”), from *wer-. Compare Middle Low German wrimpen and Lithuanian reñgtis, Latin vertō (“to turn, bend”), Old English weorpan (“to throw away”). More at versus. Beekes argues for a Pre-Greek origin.

[edit]

  • (5th BCE Attic) IPA(key): /r̥ém.bɔː/

  • (4th CE Koine) IPA(key): /ˈrem.bo/
  • (10th CE Byzantine) IPA(key): /ˈrem.bo/

Verb

[edit]

ῥέμβω • (rhémbō)

  1. to turn in circles
    • 129 CE – 216 CE, Galen, 19 134
  2. (middle voice) to roam, rove about
    • 40 CE – 115 CE, Dio Chrysostom, Collected Works 62.7
    • 342 BCE – 290 BCE, Menander 481.15
    • 46 CE – 120 CE, Plutarch, Demosthenes 6
    • 46 CE – 120 CE, Plutarch, Fabius Maximus 20
    • Ptol.Euerg. 3J
    • 300 BCE – 200 BCE, Septuagint, Proverbs 7.12
    1. (figuratively) to be unsteady, act at random
      • 46 CE – 120 CE, Plutarch, Moralia 2.80f
      • 46 CE – 120 CE, Plutarch, Pompey 20
      • 160 CE – 210 CE, Sextus Empiricus, Against the Mathematicians 2.52
