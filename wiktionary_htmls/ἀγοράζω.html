Ancient Greek

[edit]

Etymology

[edit]

From ᾰ̓γορᾱ́ (agorā́, “market”) +‎ -ᾰ́ζω (-ázō, denominative verb suffix).[1]

Pronunciation

[edit]
 
  • IPA(key): /a.ɡo.ráz.dɔː/ → /a.ɣoˈra.zo/ → /a.ɣoˈra.zo/
  • (5th BCE Attic) IPA(key): /a.ɡo.ráz.dɔː/
  • (1st CE Egyptian) IPA(key): /a.ɡoˈra.zo/
  • (4th CE Koine) IPA(key): /a.ɣoˈra.zo/
  • (10th CE Byzantine) IPA(key): /a.ɣoˈra.zo/
  • (15th CE Constantinopolitan) IPA(key): /a.ɣoˈra.zo/

Verb

[edit]

ᾰ̓γορᾰ́ζω • (agorázō)

  1. (intransitive) to be in the market, to frequent it
    • 460 BCE – 420 BCE, Herodotus, Histories 2.35
    • 460 BCE – 420 BCE, Herodotus, Histories 4.64
    • 384 BCE – 322 BCE, Aristotle, Physics 196A.5
    • Comica Adespota 710
    • 460 BCE – 395 BCE, Thucydides, History of the Peloponnesian War 6.51
  2. (transitive) to buy in the market, buy, purchase
    • 446 BCE – 386 BCE, Aristophanes, The Acharnians 625
    • 430 BCE – 354 BCE, Xenophon, Anabasis 1.5.10
    • 388 BCE, Aristophanes, Plutus 984
    • 430 BCE – 354 BCE, Xenophon, Anabasis 1.3.14
    • 384 BCE – 322 BCE, Demosthenes, Against Polycles 25
    • 384 BCE – 322 BCE, Demosthenes, Against Lacritus 19
  3. (intransitive) to haunt the market, lounge there, as a mark of idle fellows
    • 600 BCE – 300 BCE, Corinna, Collected Works 34
    • 522 BCE – 443 BCE, Pindar, Fragments 103
    • 424 BCE, Aristophanes, The Knights 1373
    • 1 CE – 200 CE, Aelius Herodianus, On Peculiar Style 2.14
