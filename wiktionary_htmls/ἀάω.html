Ancient Greek

[edit]

Etymology

[edit]

From earlier *ἀϝάω (*awáō), from Proto-Indo-European *h₂ewh₂-, though without obvious cognates.[1]

Pronunciation

[edit]
 
  • IPA(key): /a.á.ɔː/ → /aˈa.o/ → /aˈa.o/
  • (5th BCE Attic) IPA(key): /a.á.ɔː/
  • (1st CE Egyptian) IPA(key): /aˈa.o/
  • (4th CE Koine) IPA(key): /aˈa.o/
  • (10th CE Byzantine) IPA(key): /aˈa.o/
  • (15th CE Constantinopolitan) IPA(key): /aˈa.o/

Verb

[edit]

ἀάω • (aáō)

  1. (chiefly Epic) to hurt or damage the mind: mislead, infatuate (in relation to the effects of non-human things)
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Iliad 8.237
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Iliad 9.116
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Iliad 9.119
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Iliad 9.537

    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Iliad 19.136
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Iliad 19.137
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Odyssey 10.68
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Odyssey 11.61
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Odyssey 21.296
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Odyssey 21.301
