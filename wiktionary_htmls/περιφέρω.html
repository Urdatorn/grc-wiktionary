Ancient Greek

[edit]

Etymology

[edit]

    From περῐ- (peri-, “around”) +‎ φέρω (phérō, “carry”).

    Pronunciation

    [edit]
     
    • IPA(key): /pe.ri.pʰé.rɔː/ → /pe.riˈɸe.ro/ → /pe.riˈfe.ro/
    • (5th BCE Attic) IPA(key): /pe.ri.pʰé.rɔː/
    • (1st CE Egyptian) IPA(key): /pe.riˈpʰe.ro/
    • (4th CE Koine) IPA(key): /pe.riˈɸe.ro/
    • (10th CE Byzantine) IPA(key): /pe.riˈfe.ro/
    • (15th CE Constantinopolitan) IPA(key): /pe.riˈfe.ro/

    Verb

    [edit]

    περῐφέρω • (periphérō)

    1. to carry round, carry about
      • 460 BCE – 420 BCE, Herodotus, Histories 4.36
      1. to move round
        • 430 BCE – 354 BCE, Xenophon, On Horsemanship 7.2
      2. to turn round
        • 46 CE – 120 CE, Plutarch, Life of Marcellus 20
      3. to carry round, publish, make known
        • 428 BCE – 347 BCE, Plato, Protagoras 343B
      4. to carry to and fro
        • 46 CE – 120 CE, Plutarch, Life of Caesar 37
      5. to bring round (into one's own power)
        • 46 CE – 120 CE, Plutarch, Life of Pericles 15
      6. to carry round or back (in thought)
        • 460 BCE – 420 BCE, Herodotus, Histories 6.86.2
      7. to turn round, make dizzy, turn mad
        • 300 BCE – 200 BCE, Septuagint, Ecclesiastes 7.8
    2. (intransitive) to survive, endure, hold out
      • 460 BCE – 395 BCE, Thucydides, History of the Peloponnesian War 7.28
      1. to come round, recover
    3. (passive voice) to go round, revolve
      • 386 BCE – 367 BCE, Plato, Parmenides 138C
      1. to wander about
        • 430 BCE – 354 BCE, Xenophon, On Hunting 3.5
