Ancient Greek

[edit]

Alternative forms

[edit]
  • χαίνω (khaínō) — post-Classical

Etymology

[edit]

Per Rix (LIV), from Proto-Indo-European *ǵʰeh₂- (“to yawn, to gape”) with inchoative suffix -σκω (-skō). Often derived from Proto-Indo-European *ǵʰeh₁y- (“to yawn, gape”) despite the phonetic difficulties, in which case cognates include Latin hiō, Tocharian A śew, Tocharian B kāyā, Lithuanian žioti, Russian зия́ть (zijátʹ), Sanskrit विजिहीते (vijihīte), and Proto-Germanic *gīnaną. Proto-Germanic *ganōną (English yawn) has been variously derived from both roots.

Verb

[edit]

χᾰ́σκω • (kháskō)

  1. to yawn, gape, open wide
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Iliad 4.182
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Iliad 8.150
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Iliad 17.417
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Iliad 16.350
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Iliad 20.168
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Odyssey 12.350
    • 497 BCE – 405 BCE, Sophocles, Fragments 508
    • 460 BCE – 420 BCE, Herodotus, Histories 2.68
    • 4th century BC, Eubulus, Collected Works 115
    • 161 CE – 180 CE, Marcus Aurelius, Meditations 3.2
    1. (chiefly in Comic Poets) to gape in eager expectation
      • 446 BCE – 386 BCE, Aristophanes, The Acharnians 10
      • 446 BCE – 386 BCE, Aristophanes, Fragments 337
      • 4th century BC, Eubulus, Collected Works 15.11
      • 300 BCE – 200 BCE, Euphro, Collected Works 1.30
      • 423 BCE, Aristophanes, The Clouds 996
      • 424 BCE, Aristophanes, The Knights 651
      • 424 BCE, Aristophanes, The Knights 804
      • 234 CE – 305 CE, Porphyry, To Marcella 9
      • 170 CE – 250 CE, Philostratus, Life of Apollonius of Tyana 2.7
      • 423 BCE, Aristophanes, The Clouds 172
      • 380 BCE, Plato, The Republic 529B
      • 405 BCE, Aristophanes, The Frogs 990
      • 424 BCE, Aristophanes, The Knights 261
      • 422 BCE, Aristophanes, The Wasps 617
    2. to yawn from weariness, ennui or inattention
      • 446 BCE – 386 BCE, Aristophanes, The Acharnians 30
      • 424 BCE, Aristophanes, The Knights 1032
      • 425 BCE, Aristophanes, Lysistrata 426
      • 400 BCE – 300 BCE, Mnesimachus, Collected Works 4.22
  2. (less common) to speak with open mouth, to utter
    • 497 BCE – 405 BCE, Sophocles, Ajax 1227
    • 422 BCE, Aristophanes, The Wasps 342
    • 310 BCE – 240 BCE, Callimachus, Hymn to Apollo 24
  3. to open and swallow
    • 110 CE – 180 CE, Pausanias Periegeta, Graeciae Descriptio 6.21.13
