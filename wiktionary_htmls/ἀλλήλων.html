Ancient Greek

[edit]

Alternative forms

[edit]
  • ᾰ̓λλᾱ́λων (allā́lōn) — Aeolic, Doric

Etymology

[edit]

From phrases such as ἄλλος ἄλλον (állos állon), ἄλλοι ἄλλους (álloi állous, “one another”), with loss of λ either by dissimilation or by the lengthening of α, as well as restoration of the second initial vowel ή (ḗ) (< *h₂élyos (“other”)). For similar formations, compare Latin alius alium, Sanskrit अन्योन्य (anyonya, “one another, mutual”).[1]

Pronunciation

[edit]
 
  • IPA(key): /al.lɛ̌ː.lɔːn/ → /alˈli.lon/ → /aˈli.lon/
  • (5th BCE Attic) IPA(key): /al.lɛ̌ː.lɔːn/
  • (1st CE Egyptian) IPA(key): /alˈle̝.lon/
  • (4th CE Koine) IPA(key): /alˈli.lon/
  • (10th CE Byzantine) IPA(key): /alˈli.lon/
  • (15th CE Constantinopolitan) IPA(key): /aˈli.lon/

Pronoun

[edit]

ἀλλήλων • (allḗlōn)

  1. referring back to a plural subject, and expressing an action done in two directions: of one another, to one another, one another, each other, mutually, reciprocally
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Iliad 4.62–63:
      ἀλλ' ἤτοι μὲν ταῦθ' ὑποείξομεν ἀλλήλοισι, / σοὶ μὲν ἐγώ, σὺ δ’ ἐμοί·
      all' ḗtoi mèn taûth' hupoeíxomen allḗloisi, / soì mèn egṓ, sù d’ emoí;
      Anyway, let's yield herein to one another, / I to you, and you to me.
    • 472 BCE, Aeschylus, The Persians 189
    • 522 BCE – 443 BCE, Pindar, Pythian Ode 4.223
    • 460 BCE – 395 BCE, Thucydides, History of the Peloponnesian War 2.70
    • 430 BCE – 354 BCE, Xenophon, Memorabilia 4.4.23
    • 428 BCE – 347 BCE, Plato, Phaedrus 264B
    • 380 BCE, Plato, Gorgias 472C
    • 384 BCE – 322 BCE, Aristotle, Prior Analytics 57B.18
    • 384 BCE – 322 BCE, Aristotle, Prior Analytics 59A.32
    • 384 BCE – 322 BCE, Aristotle, Problems 953B.32
    • 3rd century CE, Diogenes Laertius, Lives and Opinions of Eminent Philosophers 9.89
