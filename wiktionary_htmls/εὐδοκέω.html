Ancient Greek

[edit]

Etymology

[edit]

From εὐ- (eu-, “good”) +‎ δοκέω (dokéō, “to seem, appear”).

Pronunciation

[edit]
 
  • IPA(key): /eu̯.do.ké.ɔː/ → /eβ.ðoˈce.o/ → /ev.ðoˈce.o/
  • (5th BCE Attic) IPA(key): /eu̯.do.ké.ɔː/
  • (1st CE Egyptian) IPA(key): /ew.doˈke.o/
  • (4th CE Koine) IPA(key): /eβ.ðoˈce.o/
  • (10th CE Byzantine) IPA(key): /ev.ðoˈce.o/
  • (15th CE Constantinopolitan) IPA(key): /ev.ðoˈce.o/

Verb

[edit]

εὐδοκέω • (eudokéō)

  1. to be well pleased or content, to acquiesce in a thing (with participle or dative of thing)
    • 200 BCE – 118 BCE, Polybius, The Histories 2.49.2
    • 200 BCE – 118 BCE, Polybius, The Histories 29.12.8
    • 200 BCE – 118 BCE, Polybius, The Histories 15.36.6
    • 200 BCE – 118 BCE, Polybius, The Histories 18.52.5
    • 36 BCE – 30 BCE, Diodorus Siculus, Historical Library 17.47
    • 60 BCE – 7 BCE, Dionysius of Halicarnassus, Roman Antiquities 8.74
    • New Testament, Second Epistle to the Thessalonians 2:12
    • 300 BCE – 200 BCE, Septuagint, Isaiah 62.4
    • 55 CE – 56 CE, Paul the Apostle, Second Epistle to the Corinthians 12:10
    • New Testament, Second Epistle of Peter 1:17
    • 300 BCE – 200 BCE, Septuagint, Judith 15.10
    • 300 BCE – 200 BCE, Septuagint, Genesis 33.10
    • 300 BCE – 200 BCE, Septuagint, Leviticus 26.34
  2. to be well-pleasing or acceptable, find favor with (with dative of person)
    • 200 BCE – 118 BCE, Polybius, The Histories 20.5.10
    • 100 CE – 200 CE, Maximus of Tyre, Collected Works 32.5
