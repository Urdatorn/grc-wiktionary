Ancient Greek

[edit]

Etymology

[edit]

From a reduplicated present *h₂í-h₂ews-ye- of Proto-Indo-European *h₂wes- (“pass the night”). Cognates include Old Armenian ագանիմ (aganim), Hittite 𒄷𒅖𒍣 (ḫuiš-zi, “to live, survive”), and Sanskrit वसति (vásati, “to dwell, remain, stay; to stop”). Related to αὐλή (aulḗ).

Verb

[edit]

ἰαύω • (iaúō)

  1. (poetic) to sleep, pass the night
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Iliad 14.213
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Odyssey 11.261
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Odyssey 22.464
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Odyssey 34.209
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Iliad 18.259
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Iliad 9.325
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Odyssey 19.340
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Odyssey 14.16
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Odyssey 9.184
    • 497 BCE – 405 BCE, Sophocles, Ajax 1204
    • c. 440 BCE, Euripides, Rhesus 740
    1. (with genitive) to stop, make to cease
      • 300 BCE – 200 BCE, Lycophron, Alexandra 101
      • 408 BCE, Euripides, The Phoenician Women 1537, (πόδα (póda) belongs to ἀλαίνων (alaínōn), not ἰαύων (iaúōn))
