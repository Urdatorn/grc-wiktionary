Ancient Greek

[edit]

Alternative forms

[edit]
  • πρεῖγυς (preîgus) — Cretan

Etymology

[edit]

The first component of the word is likely from Proto-Indo-European *pres (“before, in front”), an extended form of *per-. The second component is debated:[1]

  • From Proto-Indo-European *gʷṓws (“cattle”), thus "one who leads cattle".
  • From Proto-Indo-European *gʷew-, a variant of *gʷem- (“to go”), thus "one who goes in front". Compare Sanskrit पुरोगव (purogava, “leader”), from पुरस् (puras, “in front”) and गम् (gam, “to go”).
  • Together with the first component, from Proto-Indo-European *pre(y)sgʷu-, and cognate with Old Armenian երէց (erēcʻ, “elder, senior”) and Latin priscus (“ancient”), pristinus (“primitive, pristine”).

Adjective

[edit]

πρέσβῠς • (présbus) m (feminine πρέσβειᾰ, neuter πρέσβῠ); first/third declension

  1. elderly, aged
    • 429 BCE, Sophocles, Oedipus the King 941
    • 409 BCE, Sophocles, Philoctetes 665

Noun

[edit]

πρέσβῠς • (présbus) m (genitive πρέσβεως); third declension

  1. old man
  2. elder, chief, prince
    • 458 BCE, Aeschylus, Agamemnon 184,205,530
  3. (superlative) revered, honored
  4. ambassador
    • 470 BCE, Aeschylus, The Suppliants 728
  5. (at Sparta) a political title, higher than γέρων (gérōn, “senator”): chief, president
    • Inscriptiones Laconiae 1237, 1326
  6. a name of the τρόχιλος (trókhilos, “wren”)
    • 384 BCE – 322 BCE, Aristotle, History of Animals 9.11,5
  7. a type of daw or chough
    • 384 BCE – 322 BCE, Aristotle, History of Animals 9.1,15

Usage notes

[edit]

For the feminine, see πρέσβᾰ (présba), πρέσβειρᾰ (présbeira), πρεσβηῐ́ς (presbēís).
