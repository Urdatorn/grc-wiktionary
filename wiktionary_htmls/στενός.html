Ancient Greek

[edit]

Alternative forms

[edit]
  • στεινός (steinós) — Ionic
  • στέννος (sténnos) — Aeolic

Etymology

[edit]

From an earlier *στενϝός (*stenwós), of unknown further origin, with no known cognates outside Greek. The suffix of the derivative στενυγρός (stenugrós, “narrow”) suggests Pre-Greek origin.[1]

Pronunciation

[edit]
 
  • IPA(key): /ste.nós/ → /steˈnos/ → /steˈnos/
  • (5th BCE Attic) IPA(key): /ste.nós/
  • (1st CE Egyptian) IPA(key): /steˈnos/
  • (4th CE Koine) IPA(key): /steˈnos/
  • (10th CE Byzantine) IPA(key): /steˈnos/
  • (15th CE Constantinopolitan) IPA(key): /steˈnos/

Adjective

[edit]

στενός • (stenós) m (feminine στενή, neuter στενόν); first/second declension

  1. narrow, tight
    Antonym: εὐρύς (eurús)

Derived terms

[edit]
  • στείνομαι (steínomai, “to be narrowed, crowd, be crowded”)
  • στεῖνος (steînos, “narrowness, narrow room, throng, distress”)
  • στείνω (steínō, “to narrow, crowd”)
  • στενόομαι (stenóomai, “to become narrow”)
  • στενότης (stenótēs, “narrowness, tightness”)
  • στενόω (stenóō, “to make narrow”)
  • στενυγρός (stenugrós, “narrow”)
  • στενυγρῶσαι (stenugrôsai, “to contract”)
  • Στενύκληρος (Stenúklēros, “narrow piece of land”)

Greek

[edit]

Etymology

[edit]

Inherited from Ancient Greek στενός (stenós).

Adjective

[edit]

στενός • (stenós) m (feminine στενή, neuter στενό)

  1. narrow
  2. tight
  3. close
    στενός φίλος ― stenós fílos ― close friend
