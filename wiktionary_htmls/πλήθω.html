Ancient Greek

[edit]

Etymology

[edit]

From Proto-Hellenic *plḗtʰō, from Proto-Indo-European *pleh₁- with root extension like plebs. Cognates include Latin pleo, Sanskrit पिपर्ति (piparti, “to fill”) and Avestan 𐬵𐬀𐬨𐬞𐬁𐬟𐬭𐬁𐬌𐬙𐬌 (hampāfrāiti).

  • (5th BCE Attic) IPA(key): /plɛ̌ː.tʰɔː/
  • (1st CE Egyptian) IPA(key): /ˈple̝.tʰo/
  • (4th CE Koine) IPA(key): /ˈpli.θo/
  • (10th CE Byzantine) IPA(key): /ˈpli.θo/
  • (15th CE Constantinopolitan) IPA(key): /ˈpli.θo/

Verb

[edit]

πλήθω • (plḗthō)

  1. to be or become full, to swell
    • 458 BCE, Aeschylus, Agamemnon 1220
    • 472 BCE, Aeschylus, The Persians 420
    • 310 BCE – 240 BCE, Callimachus, Fr. 166
    • 300 BCE – 200 BCE, Herodas, Collected Works 7.84
    • 750 BCE – 650 BCE, Hesiod, The Shield of Heracles 478
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Iliad 5.87
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Iliad 11.492
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Iliad 18.484
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Iliad 21.218
    • 630 BCE – 570 BCE, Sappho, Collected Works 3
    • 556 BCE – 468 BCE, Simonides, Fragmenta 142.7
    • 300 BCE – 200 BCE, Theocritus, Collected Works 22.38
    1. (transitive, only in later poets)
      • 300 BCE – 250 BCE, Apollonius of Rhodes, Argonautica 3.1392
      • 300 BCE – 250 BCE, Apollonius of Rhodes, Argonautica 4.564
      • Oppian, Cynegetica 1.126
      • 300 CE – 400 CE, Quintus Smyrnaeus, Posthomerica 6.345
      • 300 CE – 400 CE, Quintus Smyrnaeus, Posthomerica 14.607
  2. to go through a period of time (from the filling of the moon)

Usage notes

[edit]

Intransitive counterpart of πίμπλημῐ (pímplēmi).
