Ancient Greek

[edit]

Etymology

[edit]

From Proto-Indo-European *-eh₂yéti. Cognates include Latin -āre (the whole first conjugation in the present) together with its Proto-Italic ancestor *-āō, -अयति (-ayati) (for the causative in Sanskrit), Proto-Celtic *-āti, Proto-Germanic *-ōną (referring again to the whole conjugation in which the infinitive is *-ōną) and Proto-Balto-Slavic *-ā́ˀtei (whence the infinite Proto-Slavic *-ati).

Pronunciation

[edit]
 
  • IPA(key): /á.ɔː/ → /ˈa.o/ → /ˈa.o/
  • (5th BCE Attic) IPA(key): /á.ɔː/
  • (1st CE Egyptian) IPA(key): /ˈa.o/
  • (4th CE Koine) IPA(key): /ˈa.o/
  • (10th CE Byzantine) IPA(key): /ˈa.o/
  • (15th CE Constantinopolitan) IPA(key): /ˈa.o/

Suffix

[edit]

-ᾰ́ω • (-áō)

  1. Forms verbs, usually from nouns in -ᾱ (-ā), -η (-ē)

Greek

[edit]

Etymology

[edit]

Modern metaplasm of the ending of 2nd Conjugation, Class I verbs in -ώ (-ó), in the pattern of the 3rd person singular -άει. Thus, frequently coinciding with an ancient uncontracted ending -άω (-áō).

Pronunciation

[edit]
  • IPA(key): /ˈa.o/

Suffix

[edit]

-άω • (-áo)

  1. modern alternative ending for 2nd Conjugation, Class I verbs in -ώ:
    αγαπώ (agapó) ("I love") → αγαπάω (agapáo)

Usage notes

[edit]
  • For 2nd Conjugation, Class I, the -άω (-áo) ending is less formal and more common than the -ώ ending. Sometimes they are interchangeable. Rarely is the -ώ ending more common.

Related terms

[edit]
  • -ώ (-ó), -ῶ (pre 1982 polytonic script for the 2nd Conjugation verb endings)
  • -ω (-o, 1st conjugation verb ending)
