Ancient Greek

[edit]

Etymology

[edit]

περῐ- (peri-, “around”) +‎ πατέω (patéō, “to walk”).[1]

Pronunciation

[edit]
 
  • IPA(key): /pe.ri.pa.té.ɔː/ → /pe.ri.paˈte.o/ → /pe.ri.paˈte.o/
  • (5th BCE Attic) IPA(key): /pe.ri.pa.té.ɔː/
  • (1st CE Egyptian) IPA(key): /pe.ri.paˈte.o/
  • (4th CE Koine) IPA(key): /pe.ri.paˈte.o/
  • (10th CE Byzantine) IPA(key): /pe.ri.paˈte.o/
  • (15th CE Constantinopolitan) IPA(key): /pe.ri.paˈte.o/

Verb

[edit]

περῐπᾰτέω • (peripatéō)

  1. to walk up and down, walk about
    • 70 CE – 110 CE, The Gospel of Matthew 9:5:
      Ἔγειρε καὶ περιπάτει
      Égeire kaì peripátei
      Get up and walk
    • 375 BCE – 275 BCE, Alexis 204
    • 50 CE – 250 CE, Apollonius Dyscolus, On Syntax 279.19
    • 424 BCE, Aristophanes, The Knights 744
    • 425 BCE, Aristophanes, Lysistrata 709
    • 422 BCE, Aristophanes, The Wasps 237
    • 342 BCE – 290 BCE, Menander, The Girl who has her hair cropped 156
    • 380 BCE, Plato, Euthydemus 273a
    • 46 CE – 120 CE, Plutarch, Moralia 2.796d
    • 430 BCE – 354 BCE, Xenophon, Memorabilia 3.13.5
  2. to discourse
    • 3rd century CE, Diogenes Laertius, Lives and Opinions of Eminent Philosophers 7.109
    • 170 CE – 250 CE, Philostratus, Life of Apollonius of Tyana 1.17
    • 170 CE – 250 CE, Philostratus, Life of Apollonius of Tyana 7.22
    • 428 BCE – 347 BCE, Plato, Epistles 348c
  3. (figuratively) to live
    • The Gospel of Mark 7:5
    • New Testament, Second Epistle to the Thessalonians 3:6
    • 110 BCE – 35 BCE, Philodemus, On Frank Criticism 12O
