Ancient Greek

[edit]

Etymology

[edit]

From σήψ (sḗps, “putrefying sore”). The alternation between η and α has suggested PIE origin (possible roots: Proto-Indo-European *tweh₂p-, *tyeh₂p- or *Kyeh₂p- (last option chosen by LIV)), though a zero grade -ih₂- would not actually yield Proto-Greek -ya-. Beekes rejects relation to Lithuanian siupti (“to putrefy”) or Sanskrit क्याकु (kyāku, “fungus”).

Verb

[edit]

σήπω • (sḗpō)

  1. (active voice) to make rotten or putrid
    • 175 CE – 235 CE, Claudius Aelianus, On the Nature of Animals 16.40
    • 525 BCE – 455 BCE, Aeschylus, Fragments 275
    • 458 BCE, Aeschylus, The Libation Bearers 995
    1. (figuratively) to corrupt, waste
      • 60 BCE – 7 BCE, Dionysius of Halicarnassus, Roman Antiquities 11.37
      • 366 BCE – 348 BCE, Plato, Theaetetus 153c
  2. (passive voice) to rot, moulder
    • 424 BCE, Aristophanes, The Knights 1308
    • 460 BCE – 420 BCE, Herodotus, Histories 2.41
    • 750 BCE – 650 BCE, Hesiod, The Shield of Heracles 152
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Iliad 2.135
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Iliad 19.27
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Iliad 24.414
    1. (of tissue) to die
      • 458 BCE, Euripides, Electra 319
      • 460 BCE – 420 BCE, Herodotus, Histories 3.66
      • 460 BCE – 420 BCE, Herodotus, Histories 6.136
      • 428 BCE – 347 BCE, Plato, Phaedo 80d
