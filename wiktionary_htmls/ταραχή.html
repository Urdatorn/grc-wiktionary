Ancient Greek

[edit]

Etymology

[edit]

From ταράσσω (tarássō, “to disturb, stir, agitate”) +‎ -ή (-ḗ).

Pronunciation

[edit]
 
  • IPA(key): /ta.ra.kʰɛ̌ː/ → /ta.raˈçi/ → /ta.raˈçi/
  • (5th BCE Attic) IPA(key): /ta.ra.kʰɛ̌ː/
  • (1st CE Egyptian) IPA(key): /ta.raˈkʰe̝/
  • (4th CE Koine) IPA(key): /ta.raˈçi/
  • (10th CE Byzantine) IPA(key): /ta.raˈçi/
  • (15th CE Constantinopolitan) IPA(key): /ta.raˈçi/

Noun

[edit]

τᾰρᾰχή • (tarakhḗ) f (genitive τᾰρᾰχῆς); first declension

  1. disorder, disturbance
  2. confusion (mental)

Greek

[edit]

Etymology

[edit]

Inherited from Ancient Greek ταραχή (tarakhḗ).[1]

Pronunciation

[edit]
  • IPA(key): /ta.ɾaˈçi/
  • Hyphenation: τα‧ρα‧χή

Noun

[edit]

ταραχή • (tarachí) f (plural ταραχές)

  1. agitation
  2. disturbance, unrest, turmoil
    Synonym: αναταραχή f (anatarachí)
  3. (in the plural) disorder, disturbances, unrest (interruption of civic peace or of public order)
