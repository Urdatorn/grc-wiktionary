Ancient Greek

[edit]

Alternative forms

[edit]
  • ῐ̔λᾰ́ομαι (hiláomai) — Epic

Etymology

[edit]

From the reduplicated *si-sl̥h₂-sḱetor, from Proto-Indo-European *selh₂- (“mercy, comfort”). By surface analysis, ῑ̔́λαος (hī́laos, “propitious, gracious”) +‎ -σκω (-skō).

Directly cognate to Old Armenian աղաչեմ (ałačʻem, “to pray”), and further to Latin sōlor (“to comfort”), Gothic 𐍃𐌴𐌻𐍃 (sēls, “good, kind”), Icelandic sæll (“happy”), German selig (“overjoyed”), English silly.

Verb

[edit]

ῑ̔λᾰ́σκομαι • (hīláskomai)

  1. to appease, conciliate, propitiate; to expiate
    • 460 BCE – 420 BCE, Herodotus, Histories 5.47
    • 460 BCE – 420 BCE, Herodotus, Histories 8.112
    • 750 BCE – 650 BCE, Hesiod, Works and Days 338
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Iliad 1.100
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Iliad 1.147
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Iliad 1.386
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Iliad 1.472
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Odyssey 3.419
    • 342 BCE – 290 BCE, Menander, Men at Arbitration 558
    • New Testament, Epistle to the Hebrews 2:17
    • 522 BCE – 443 BCE, Pindar, Olympian Ode 7.9
    • 46 CE – 120 CE, Plutarch, Cato the Younger 61
  2. (passive voice) to be merciful, gracious
    • 50 CE – 100 CE, The Gospel of Luke 18:13
    • 300 BCE – 200 BCE, Septuagint, 2 Kings 5.18
    • 300 BCE – 200 BCE, Septuagint, Exodus 32.14
    • 300 BCE – 200 BCE, Septuagint, Psalms 77.38
