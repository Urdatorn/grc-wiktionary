Ancient Greek

[edit]

Etymology

[edit]

Beekes suggests a derivation from Proto-Indo-European *h₁ergʰ- (“to mount”). According to Frisk, an iterative-intensive formation from ἔρχομαι (érkhomai) ("come, go"). Watkins posits a connection with ὄρχις (órkhis) ("testicle").

Pronunciation

[edit]
 
  • IPA(key): /or.kʰé.o.mai̯/ → /orˈçe.o.mɛ/ → /orˈçe.o.me/
  • (5th BCE Attic) IPA(key): /or.kʰé.o.mai̯/
  • (1st CE Egyptian) IPA(key): /orˈkʰe.o.mɛ/
  • (4th CE Koine) IPA(key): /orˈçe.o.mɛ/
  • (10th CE Byzantine) IPA(key): /orˈçe.o.me/
  • (15th CE Constantinopolitan) IPA(key): /orˈçe.o.me/

Verb

[edit]

ὀρχέομαι • (orkhéomai)

  1. to dance
    • 408 BCE – 334 BCE, Antiphanes Comicus, Collected Works 113.1
    • 250 CE – 350 CE, Athenaeus, Deipnosophistae 4.155b
    • 250 CE – 350 CE, Athenaeus, Deipnosophistae 14.631d
    • Euph. 87
    • 460 BCE – 420 BCE, Herodotus, Histories 1.66
    • 750 BCE – 650 BCE, Hesiod, Theogony 4
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Iliad 18.594
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Odyssey 8.371
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Odyssey 14.465
    • 430 BCE – 354 BCE, Xenophon, Cyropaedia 1.3.10
    • 430 BCE – 354 BCE, Xenophon, Cyropaedia 8.4.12
    • 430 BCE – 354 BCE, Xenophon, Symposium 7.5
  2. to represent by dancing or pantomime
    • 125 CE – 200 CE, Lucian, The Dance 80
  3. (figuratively) to leap, bound
    • 458 BCE, Aeschylus, The Libation Bearers 166
    • 310 BCE – 240 BCE, Callimachus, Del. 139
  4. (active voice) to cause to dance
