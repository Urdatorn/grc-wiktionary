Ancient Greek

[edit]

Etymology

[edit]

Variant of νή (nḗ, “yea”).[1][2]

Pronunciation

[edit]
 
  • IPA(key): /nǎi̯/ → /nɛ/ → /ne/
  • (5th BCE Attic) IPA(key): /nǎi̯/
  • (1st CE Egyptian) IPA(key): /nɛ/
  • (4th CE Koine) IPA(key): /nɛ/
  • (10th CE Byzantine) IPA(key): /ne/
  • (15th CE Constantinopolitan) IPA(key): /ne/

Adverb

[edit]

ναί • (naí)

  1. (used to express strong affirmation) yea, verily
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Iliad 1.286
    • 490 BCE – 430 BCE, Empedocles, Collected Works 76.2
    • 300 BCE – 200 BCE, Theocritus, Collected Works 27.27
    • 300 BCE – 250 BCE, Apollonius of Rhodes, Argonautica 2.151
    • 125 CE – 200 CE, Lucian, Astrology 14
    • 497 BCE – 405 BCE, Sophocles, Electra 1445
    1. (in oaths)
      • 422 BCE, Aristophanes, The Wasps 1438
      • 430 BCE – 354 BCE, Xenophon, Hellenica 4.4.10
      • 431 BCE, Euripides, Medea 1277
  2. (in answers) yes
    • 472 BCE, Aeschylus, The Persians 738
    • 366 BCE – 348 BCE, Plato, Theaetetus 193A
    • 380 BCE, Plato, Gorgias 448B
    • 423 BCE, Aristophanes, The Clouds 1468
    • 70 CE – 110 CE, The Gospel of Matthew 5:37
    1. (with ἀλλά (allá, “but”) or similar words, as a qualified assent) yes...but
      • 389 BCE – 314 BCE, Aeschines, Against Ctesiphon 84
      • 360 BCE, Plato, The Sophist 226E
      • 380 BCE, Plato, The Republic 415E

Antonyms

[edit]
  • οὐ (ou)
