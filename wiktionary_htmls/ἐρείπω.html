Ancient Greek

[edit]

Alternative forms

[edit]
  • ἐριπόω (eripóō)
  • ἐρίπτω (eríptō)

Etymology

[edit]

From Proto-Indo-European *h₁reyp- (“to tear”). Cognate with Latin rīpa (“river bank”), Old Norse rīfa (“tear out”).[1]

Pronunciation

[edit]
 
  • IPA(key): /e.rěː.pɔː/ → /eˈri.po/ → /eˈri.po/
  • (5th BCE Attic) IPA(key): /e.rěː.pɔː/
  • (1st CE Egyptian) IPA(key): /eˈri.po/
  • (4th CE Koine) IPA(key): /eˈri.po/
  • (10th CE Byzantine) IPA(key): /eˈri.po/
  • (15th CE Constantinopolitan) IPA(key): /eˈri.po/

Verb

[edit]

ἐρείπω • (ereípō)

  1. to throw or dash down, tear down
    • 500 BCE – 400 BCE, Bacchylides, Collected Works 10.68
    • 460 BCE – 420 BCE, Herodotus, Histories 1.164
    • 750 BCE – 650 BCE, Hesiod, Theogony 704
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Iliad 12.258
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Iliad 14.15
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Iliad 15.361
    • 522 BCE – 443 BCE, Pindar, Olympian Ode 2.43
    • 46 CE – 120 CE, Plutarch, Alexander the Great 33
    • 200 BCE – 118 BCE, Polybius, The Histories 1.42.10
    • 497 BCE – 405 BCE, Sophocles, Ajax 309
    • 442 BCE, Sophocles, Antigone 596
    • 406 BCE, Sophocles, Oedipus at Colonus 1373
    • 406 BCE, Sophocles, Oedipus at Colonus 1462
  2. (intransitive) to fall down
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Iliad 5.47
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Iliad 11.743
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Iliad 13.389
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Iliad 21.243
    • 300 BCE – 200 BCE, Theocritus, Collected Works 13.50
