Ancient Greek

[edit]

Etymology

[edit]

From ἐκ- (ek-, “out”) +‎ ῥέω (rhéō, “flow”).

Pronunciation

[edit]
 
  • IPA(key): /e.kré.ɔː/ → /eˈkre.o/ → /eˈkre.o/
  • (5th BCE Attic) IPA(key): /e.kré.ɔː/
  • (1st CE Egyptian) IPA(key): /eˈkre.o/
  • (4th CE Koine) IPA(key): /eˈkre.o/
  • (10th CE Byzantine) IPA(key): /eˈkre.o/
  • (15th CE Constantinopolitan) IPA(key): /eˈkre.o/

Verb

[edit]

ἐκρέω • (ekréō)

  1. to flow out or forth
    • 460 BCE – 420 BCE, Herodotus, Histories 2.20
    • 460 BCE – 420 BCE, Herodotus, Histories 2.149
    • 800 BCE – 600 BCE, Homer, Iliad 21.119
    • 428 BCE – 347 BCE, Plato, Phaedo 112a
    1. (of feathers) to fall off
      • 414 BCE, Aristophanes, The Birds 104
      • 384 BCE – 322 BCE, Aristotle, History of Animals 518a.32
    2. to shed fruit
      • 300 BCE – 200 BCE, Septuagint, Deuteronomy 28.40
    3. (figuratively) to melt or fall away, to disappear
      • 380 BCE, Plato, The Republic 452d
      • 428 BCE – 347 BCE, Plato, Theages 130e
      • 46 CE – 120 CE, Plutarch, Themistocles 12
  2. (with cognate accusative) to shed, let fall
